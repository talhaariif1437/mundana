Journey to Petra Unlocking the Ancient Rose-Red City

Step into a world carved in stone, where ancient history meets breathtaking beauty in the heart of Southern Jordan.

Jane Seymour A few hours ago · 5 min. read
Share this

Petra, located in southern Jordan, is one of the world’s most famous archaeological sites and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Known as the "Rose City" because of the distinctive red sandstone cliffs from which many of its structures are carved, Petra was the capital of the ancient Nabataean Kingdom, flourishing between the 4th century BCE and 2nd century CE. It is renowned for its elaborate rock-cut architecture and sophisticated water management system.

Key Points


1. Al-Khazneh (The Treasury): Petra’s most iconic structure, Al-Khazneh is a grand temple or mausoleum, carved into the rose-red sandstone cliff face. Its stunning facade, with intricate carvings, has made it one of the most photographed landmarks in the world. The Treasury was likely the tomb of a Nabataean king, although its original purpose remains a subject of debate.

2. Siq: The Siq is a narrow, winding gorge that serves as the main entrance to Petra. Stretching for about 1.2 kilometers (0.75 miles), the Siq is flanked by towering rock walls that rise up to 80 meters (260 feet) high. As visitors walk through it, they are gradually led to the Treasury, a dramatic reveal that showcases Petra’s unique beauty.

3. The Royal Tombs: Located on the opposite side of the Siq, the Royal Tombs are a collection of large burial chambers carved into the cliffs. The facades are designed to resemble classical Greek and Roman architecture, showcasing the Nabataeans' blending of various cultural influences. The most famous tombs here are the Urn Tomb, Silk Tomb, and Corinthian Tomb.

4. The Monastery (Ad Deir): The Monastery is one of Petra's largest and most impressive structures, similar in design to the Treasury, but even larger in scale. It is carved into the side of a mountain and is accessible via a steep climb of around 800 steps. The view from the top offers a breathtaking panorama of the surrounding desert and valleys.

5. The Roman-style Theater: Built in the 1st century CE, Petra’s Roman-style theater could hold up to 3,000 spectators. It is carved directly into the rock and is thought to have been used for public performances and gatherings.

6. The Great Temple and Colonnaded Street: The Great Temple is an expansive site located near the center of Petra. It likely served as a religious and administrative center. The Colonnaded Street, once the main thoroughfare of Petra, is lined with columns and connects key parts of the city.

7. Water Systems: The Nabataeans were skilled engineers, and their advanced water management system enabled them to thrive in the arid desert environment. They built an extensive network of dams, cisterns, and aqueducts that allowed them to collect and store water, ensuring the survival of the city even in dry periods.

History & Significance


Petra was founded by the Nabataeans, a nomadic Arab people who settled in the region around the 6th century BCE. The city flourished as a center of trade due to its strategic location between the Arabian Peninsula, Egypt, and the Mediterranean. Petra became an important hub for the trade of spices, incense, silk, and other luxury goods, and it prospered under Nabataean control.

In the 2nd century CE, Petra was absorbed into the Roman Empire, and its influence declined. The city eventually fell into obscurity, especially after an earthquake in 363 CE and a shift in trade routes. Petra remained largely forgotten by the outside world until its rediscovery by Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt in 1812.

Today, Petra is one of Jordan's most popular tourist attractions, drawing visitors from around the globe to marvel at its architectural wonders, history, and the stunning desert landscape. It was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007, solidifying its place as one of the greatest archaeological treasures of the ancient world.
