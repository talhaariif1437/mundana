"Israel-Palestine Conflict, A Century of Struggle and Resilience"

"Unpacking the Historical Roots, Key Events, and Complex Dynamics of One of the World's Most Prolonged Conflicts"

Jane Seymour A few hours ago · 5 min. read
Share this

Israel-Palestine conflict is one of the most enduring and complex conflicts in modern history. It has its roots in deep historical, religious, national, and political issues that span over a century. The war, and the broader conflict, involves competing nationalist movements, territorial disputes, and differing historical narratives. Below is an overview of the situation, highlighting the key events, causes, and dynamics.

1. Historical Background


Ottoman Empire Era: Before World War I, the area known as Palestine was part of the Ottoman Empire, inhabited mainly by Arab Muslims, with smaller Christian and Jewish populations.

Balfour Declaration (1917): During World War I, Britain issued the Balfour Declaration, supporting the establishment of a "national home for the Jewish people" in Palestine. This was seen as a promise to the Jewish community in Europe but angered the local Arab population who feared the loss of their land and sovereignty.

British Mandate: After the war, the League of Nations granted Britain control over Palestine (the British Mandate for Palestine) from 1920 to 1948. During this period, tensions between Jewish and Arab communities grew, as Jewish immigration to Palestine increased.

2. Formation of Israel and the 1948 War


UN Partition Plan (1947): As tensions escalated, the United Nations proposed a partition plan to divide Palestine into two states: one for Jews and one for Arabs. Jerusalem was to be administered internationally. The Jewish leadership accepted the plan, but the Arab states and Palestinian leaders rejected it.

Declaration of the State of Israel (1948): On May 14, 1948, following the expiration of the British mandate, David Ben-Gurion declared the independence of the state of Israel. The next day, neighboring Arab states (Egypt, Jordan, Syria, Lebanon, and Iraq) launched a military intervention to prevent the establishment of the Jewish state.

1948 Arab-Israeli War: The war resulted in a victory for Israel. By the end of the conflict, Israel had expanded beyond the borders proposed by the UN partition plan, and about 750,000 Palestinian Arabs were displaced, becoming refugees. This event is known as Nakba ("catastrophe" in Arabic) for Palestinians.

3. The Six-Day War (1967)


In 1967, rising tensions led to the Six-Day War between Israel and its neighboring Arab states. Israel launched a preemptive strike, and within six days, Israel captured large swaths of land:

  • The West Bank (including East Jerusalem) from Jordan.
  • The Gaza Strip and Sinai Peninsula from Egypt.
  • The Golan Heights from Syria.
The outcome of the war left Palestinian territories (the West Bank and Gaza Strip) under Israeli military occupation, significantly altering the territorial landscape of the conflict. East Jerusalem was also annexed by Israel, a move that is not internationally recognized.

4. The Rise of the Palestinian Liberation Organization (PLO)


After the 1967 war, the Palestinian Liberation Organization (PLO), led by Yasser Arafat, became the main political and military representative of the Palestinian people. The PLO sought to establish an independent Palestinian state, using both diplomacy and armed struggle.

In the 1970s and 1980s, the PLO conducted numerous attacks against Israel, and Israel, in turn, engaged in military operations against the group.

5. The Oslo Accords (1993)


A breakthrough in the conflict came in 1993, when Israel and the PLO signed the Oslo Accords, a series of agreements that aimed to establish a framework for peace.

The Oslo Accords allowed for the creation of the Palestinian Authority (PA) to govern parts of the West Bank and Gaza Strip, with the eventual goal of establishing a Palestinian state. Yasser Arafat and Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin were awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for their roles.

However, the peace process faltered, with continued violence, Israeli settlement expansion, and disputes over the status of Jerusalem, the return of refugees, and borders.

6. The Second Intifada (2000-2005)


In the early 2000s, frustration over the lack of progress in peace negotiations and continued Israeli occupation led to the Second Intifada (Palestinian uprising). This period was marked by suicide bombings, Israeli military operations, and significant civilian casualties on both sides.

The violence further deepened mistrust and hardened positions on both sides.

7. Hamas and the Gaza Strip


In 2006, Hamas, an Islamist militant group, won the Palestinian legislative elections, and took control of the Gaza Strip in 2007 after violent clashes with the Palestinian Authority, which is dominated by the Fatah party.

Since then, Hamas has governed Gaza, while the Palestinian Authority controls parts of the West Bank.

Israel considers Hamas a terrorist organization and has engaged in multiple military operations in Gaza to counter Hamas rocket attacks. The most notable operations include Operation Cast Lead (2008-2009), Operation Pillar of Defense (2012), Operation Protective Edge (2014), and more recent skirmishes.

8. Key Issues in the Conflict


Territorial Disputes: The core issue is the territory of historic Palestine, with both Jews and Palestinians claiming the same land. This includes disputes over the West Bank, Gaza, and East Jerusalem.

Jerusalem: Both Israel and the Palestinians claim Jerusalem as their capital. Israel controls all of Jerusalem, including East Jerusalem, which was occupied in 1967 and later annexed, though this annexation is not recognized by most of the international community.

Refugees: Palestinians who were displaced in 1948 (and their descendants) demand the right to return to their homes in what is now Israel. Israel argues that allowing this would threaten the Jewish character of the state.

Israeli Settlements: The Israeli government has built numerous settlements in the West Bank and East Jerusalem, which are considered illegal under international law, though Israel disputes this. These settlements complicate the possibility of a contiguous Palestinian state.

Security Concerns: Israel cites concerns over security from Hamas and other militant groups, leading to strict border controls, military presence, and periodic blockades, especially on Gaza.

Two-State Solution: The internationally favored solution for peace is a two-state solution, where an independent Palestinian state exists alongside Israel. However, disagreements over borders, Jerusalem, refugees, and security arrangements have stalled meaningful progress.

9. Recent Developments


Normalization of Relations: In recent years, some Arab countries, like the United Arab Emirates (UAE), Bahrain, and Morocco, have normalized relations with Israel under the Abraham Accords, brokered by the United States. This has shifted some regional dynamics, with a focus on countering Iran.

Violence in Gaza: The Gaza Strip has seen repeated cycles of violence, with rocket attacks from Hamas and Israeli airstrikes, resulting in significant loss of life and widespread destruction.

Palestinian Divisions: The split between Hamas (which controls Gaza) and the Palestinian Authority (which governs parts of the West Bank) has further complicated peace efforts, as the two factions disagree on how to achieve Palestinian national aspirations.

Conclusion


The Israel-Palestine conflict remains deeply entrenched, with historical grievances, national aspirations, and territorial disputes at the heart of the issue. The conflict continues to affect the lives of millions of people in the region, and while there have been efforts at peace, the path to a lasting and just resolution seems uncertain.
